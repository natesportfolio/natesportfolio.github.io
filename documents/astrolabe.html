This project involved the creation of astrolabes, devices used to measure the angle between you and the top of an object in order to calculate its height, from materials found around the classroom. We were then supposed to go outside and measure some nearby buildings, do the calculations and then compare our results with the results of a Google search and reflect upon our accuracy. Objective:

        To use everyday materials to construct an astrolabe, in order to approximate the heights of certain structures using the Tan principle of trigonometry.

Background:

The astrolabe is an ancient machine that was used for calculations relating to the time and the sun, and for navigational purposes using the stars for plotting positions and courses. They can also be used for plotting the heights and relative distances of objects, provided you know one or the other.

Procedure:

        In order to make a rudimentary astrolabe, you must print a template for one online. It would look like half of a protractor, and would have all of the angles marked. You must then cut out the template using scissors.

        You should attach the cut-out template to a cardboard backing in order to ensure that the paper does not flop around and present a challenge when measuring the angle. You must then create a hole in the top-right of the template.

        Cut a length of string and tie a weight to one end. Put the string through the hole, and tie knots in the open end until it won’t go through the hole.

        Attach a straw to the top edge of the astrolabe and you are done.

        In order to utilize this astrolabe, you must walk outside and find a building to measure. A tall building would work better than a short one. Look through the straw at the top of the building and have someone read out the angle that the string is at. If you would like to calculate the height of the building, you need to know how far away it is and how high your eyes are. If you would like to calculate the relative distance of the building, you need to know how tall it is.

        The Goldman Sachs Building is actually 238 meters tall, versus the 167.68 meters that was calculated. The angle should have been measured would have been 34º, but was instead 26º. This can be attributed to ambient breeze, and the astrolabe not having single angle markers, but rather markers for every 10 degrees, so the angle cannot be measured precisely. The Goldman Sachs Building may also be at a different elevation than the point at which the angle was measured, which will have an impact on the angle.

        The Statue of Liberty is 93 meters tall, versus the 181 meters that was calculated. This can be attributed to the Statue of Liberty being 2.59 kilometers away, and the fact that due to that a determinable angle was difficult to reach. The Statue of Liberty may also be at a different elevation to the point at which it was measured, and at that distance the curvature of the Earth may play a role in changing the outcome of the calculation.
